15 years experience.

I had a straight-forward entrance into IT through college. What happened after that was unexpected. I felt more comfortable talking about technology than being paid to build it, so I shifted, unintentionally, into a career in marketing. My job these days is to engage, inspire, and communicate about technology effectively. It's challenging, and it regularly requires that I understand my audiences (IT operators, developers) and my company (sales objectives, business model). I continue to stay in tech because it allows me to afford a great life for my family and gives me the flexibility I want in my life (remote work being a big part of that). I've worked at 10 person startups and 100,000 person enterprises, and I'm happy to talk to you about both.

When it comes to advice, the one must do is to find your community. Whether that is a best friend who is motivated to learn with you or an international group centered on a type of technology, find people to grow with and learn from regularly. It's an essential part of the work, and it makes it a whole lot more fun. Some of my favorites are:

  • Open Source community through Opensource.com, GitHub, or sub-communities like the OSI subreddit
  • Python community through local meetups and the Python Software Foundation
  • DevOps community through DevOpsDays and HangOps
